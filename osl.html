Bionic Leg Impedance Controller

Robust Digital Control for Soft Robotics

Series Elastic Actuator Testbed

Motivation

To emulate the behavior of a biological leg, a robotic prosthesis like the OSL must simultaneously accomplish two tasks:

1. Accurately track nominal joint trajectories for any given activity (e.g. normal walking, stair descent, running)

2. Exhibit a mechanical impedance that mimics the biological joint, thereby allowing the leg to respond naturally to unexpected perturbations (e.g. slipping on ice)

To achieve both goals in the presence of disturbances and non-linearities caused by the OSL's economic design, a series elastic actuator (SEA) combined with a disturbance observer can be used.

Contribution

Given the variety of tradeoffs and complexities associated with introducing a SEA control framework on a prosthesis, controllers are perfected on a test bed prior to human subject testing. My role was to model, simulate and test a disturbance observer control algorithm first in software, and then subsequently on the embedded system. Code was written using Python in a Linux development environment, and the system was modeled using a discrete time state space representation.

Block diagram of discrete time disturbance observer (DOB)

Results

Preliminary results show successful tracking of knee angles and torques despite excessive friction caused by the OSL's 50:1 transmission. Of particular interest, torque error was reduced to Â± 3.5 Nm which is crucial for allowing amputees to swing their leg forward with natural pendulum dynamics as they would with a passive prosthesis. Additionally, due to the successful implementation of a disturbance observer in lieu of more classical methods like PID control, the groundwork has been laid for an outer layer of compliance/impedance control capable of realizing biological damping and stiffness.
